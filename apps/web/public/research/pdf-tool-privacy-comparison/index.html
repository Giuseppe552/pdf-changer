Where Do Your PDFs Go?

Every month, hundreds of millions of people upload PDFs to free online tools. Most don't think about where those files actually go.

We tested three popular approaches to PDF processing — two cloud-based tools and one browser-based tool — and recorded every network request the browser made. No speculation. Just traffic logs.

What we tested

  • iLovePDF — the most popular free PDF tool (217M monthly visits)
  • Smallpdf — the second most popular (60M+ monthly visits)
  • PDF Changer — a browser-only tool that processes files locally (this project)

For each tool, we loaded the merge page, uploaded two small test PDFs, and captured every network request using a browser extension that intercepts traffic at the browser level. The extension runs in a privileged context that page scripts cannot interfere with.

We're including PDF Changer in the comparison because we built it and we should hold ourselves to the same scrutiny.

iLovePDF: 91 requests, 18 domains

Your files are uploaded to a dynamically assigned server (api25.ilovepdf.com in our test). The form data includes your original filenames in cleartext: files[0][filename], files[1][filename]. The server assigns its own filenames, confirming server-side storage.

Google Analytics (property G-44KQ8HETWT) receives 7 requests containing your screen resolution, browser version, OS, language, and the full page URL. A persistent client ID links visits across sessions.

iLovePDF runs its own analytics beacons to evt.ilovepdf.com tracking which tool you used, your screen dimensions, and referrer.

The download page loads Google Ad Manager, Google Ad Syndication, OpenX, and Criteo. Ad network scripts running on a page that just processed your documents.

Domains contacted by iLovePDF: www.ilovepdf.com (34), region1.google-analytics.com (7), securepubads.g.doubleclick.net (6), pagead2.googlesyndication.com (6), ep2.adtrafficquality.google (5), ep1.adtrafficquality.google (4), evt.ilovepdf.com (3), api25.ilovepdf.com (3), cm.g.doubleclick.net (2), oajs.openx.net (2), gum.criteo.com (2), and 7 others.

iLovePDF states files are deleted within 2 hours. They are ISO 27001 certified. These are their stated policies — we are not disputing them. We are documenting what the browser observes.

Smallpdf: 215 requests, 21 domains

More than twice the network traffic of iLovePDF for the same operation. 57 requests to www.google.com alone. Files uploaded to files-upload.r2.smallpdf.com (Cloudflare R2 storage). 68 POST requests with body data.

Smallpdf states files are removed after one hour for free users. They use AES-256 encryption and are ISO 27001 certified. Their trust center also states data is "only accessed in limited ways, such as responding to support requests or addressing technical issues" — confirming staff access is technically possible.

Domains contacted by Smallpdf: www.google.com (57), pluto.smallpdf.com (52), s.smallpdf.com (46), region1.google-analytics.com (7), pagead2.googlesyndication.com (7), www.googletagmanager.com (5), and 15 others.

PDF Changer: 18 requests, 1 domain

18 requests. All to pdfchanger.org. Zero third-party domains. Zero file uploads. Zero analytics. Zero ads.

The 18 requests are the HTML page, CSS, JavaScript, fonts, icons, and manifest. The assets to load the tool. Nothing else.

Processing runs inside a sandboxed iframe with connect-src 'none' — the browser physically prevents network requests from the processing context. PDF Changer also works offline.

Our tool is not perfect. The audit system is self-attested. There are no E2E browser tests. Limitations are documented on the colophon page.

The business model question

This isn't just technical. It's economic.

Cloud PDF tools process your files on their servers. Servers cost money. That cost is funded by ads and subscriptions. The ad networks on their pages aren't a bug — they're the business model. Your file processing subsidises ad impressions.

PDF Changer processes files in your browser. No servers to maintain beyond static hosting. Infrastructure costs are near zero. No ads because there's no ad revenue to pursue. Every feature is free.

This isn't a moral judgment. Both iLovePDF and Smallpdf are legitimate businesses with real certifications. The question is whether you're comfortable with the trade-off.

Verify it yourself

Open any PDF tool. Open Chrome DevTools (F12). Go to the Network tab. Process a file. Count the requests. Read the domains.

If our data is wrong, anyone can check.
